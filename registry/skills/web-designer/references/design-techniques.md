# Design Techniques

Principles and techniques for creating distinctive, award-worthy designs. For real-world site inspiration, browse Awwwards during the research phase — don't rely on memorized examples.

---

## Visual Storytelling

- Every page tells a story. The scroll IS the narrative arc: hook → build → reveal → resolve
- Pacing matters — alternate between dense information and breathing room
- Use full-viewport sections as "chapter breaks" for dramatic emphasis
- Let the content dictate the structure, not the other way around
- The first viewport is the hook — it sets the entire tone and must earn the scroll

## Spatial Composition

- **Asymmetric layouts** create tension and visual interest — avoid symmetry as default
- **Breaking the grid** intentionally draws the eye (an image bleeding past its column, text overlapping a section boundary)
- **Negative space** is a design element — generous whitespace signals quality and confidence
- **Viewport-aware sections** (100vh) create immersive, focused moments
- **Depth layering** — overlapping elements, z-index play, parallax, creates richness without clutter
- **Directional flow** — guide the eye with diagonal lines, gradient direction, or element placement

## Typography as Design

- Font choice sets the entire personality — choose first, design second
- **Contrast pairing**: serif headlines + sans body (or vice versa) creates instant sophistication
- **Scale contrast**: enormous display text (8-12vw) next to small body text creates drama
- **Fluid typography** (clamp/vw-based) feels alive and responsive
- **Monospace accents** signal technical precision — use for data, code, timestamps
- One font is often enough — use weight and size contrast instead of a second face
- **Vertical text** and **rotated labels** can break monotony in navigation or section markers

## Color Psychology

- **Moody dark themes**: not just dark gray — use deep navy, charcoal brown, forest green as base
- **Duotone palettes**: two colors + white/black creates bold, memorable identity
- **Gradient storytelling**: gradients can guide the eye and create depth (not just decoration)
- **Accent restraint**: one pop color used sparingly hits harder than rainbow
- **Color-as-navigation**: different sections/categories identified by color creates wayfinding
- **Muted with one vivid accent** — earthy/desaturated palette with a single electric highlight
- **Temperature contrast** — warm and cool colors in the same palette create visual tension

## Layout Archetypes

- **Editorial flow**: narrow text column with images breaking out to full-bleed
- **Bento grid**: mixed-size tiles, playful and modern, great for features/portfolios
- **Alternating splits**: 50/50 or 60/40 horizontal sections, flipping sides each row
- **Single column focus**: constrained width, generous typography, zero distraction
- **Dense data**: full viewport, compact rows, information-first, power-user aesthetic
- **Stacked full-bleed**: each section is a full-width world with its own background treatment
- **Overlapping layers**: elements that break section boundaries create depth and connection
- **Asymmetric grid**: unequal column widths that create visual rhythm and hierarchy

## Navigation Craft

- Navigation should match the content's energy — a minimal portfolio needs different nav than a dense dashboard
- **Transparent overlays** on hero sections create immersion
- **Icon rails** (collapsed sidebars) for complex apps — full sidebar is often overkill
- **Hidden navigation** (scroll-triggered, hamburger) forces simpler IA and rewards it
- **Breadcrumb-driven** for deep content (docs, ecommerce) — shows location without sidebar weight
- **Sticky nav that transforms** — large on load, compact on scroll

## Interaction and Motion Principles

- Motion should communicate, not decorate — enter/exit states, state changes, scroll progress
- **Hover reveals** — show secondary info on hover without cluttering the default state
- **Scroll-driven reveals** — content appearing as you reach it creates discovery
- Transitions between sections should feel intentional (fade, slide, parallax)
- Loading states and skeleton screens are part of the design
- **Micro-interactions** on buttons, toggles, and form elements signal quality

## Component Composition

- Components from the toolkit can be combined freely — a stat card inside a bento tile, a testimonial inside a hero, pricing inside a sidebar
- **Cards are not the only container** — try: plain text blocks, bordered sections, background-color shifts, indentation
- **Break component monotony**: if you have 6 feature items, don't make them all identical cards — vary the presentation (2 large + 4 small, or alternating image/text pairs)
- **Mix content types** within sections — text, images, stats, quotes side by side
- **Nested layouts** — a grid within a card, a timeline within a split section

## What Makes a Design Award-Worthy

Based on Awwwards judging criteria:

- **Design (40%)**: Is there a clear visual system? Does every element feel intentional? Is the typography refined? Does the color palette create mood and harmony? Are complex ideas turned into simple, beautiful interfaces?
- **Usability (30%)**: Can you navigate without thinking? Is the hierarchy scannable? Is contrast accessible? Is the information architecture logical?
- **Creativity (20%)**: Would a jury member say "I haven't seen this before"? Does it break expectations while remaining functional? Is there a unique structural idea or visual treatment?
- **Content (10%)**: Does the content feel real and integrated, not pasted onto a template? Is the density appropriate? Does the design serve the content or fight it?

**The creativity test**: if you could swap the content and the design would work for any other project, it's not creative enough. The design should feel inseparable from what it's presenting.
